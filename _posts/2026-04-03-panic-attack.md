title: Panic attack
section: write
---
A feeling of lightheadedness and impending failure stops me in the kitchen. My breathing is shallow. I've pulled out the bag of coffee beans, the water pitcher, Greek yogurt, and blueberries from the fridge. 

*Breathe.*

I pour some water from the pitcher into the electric kettle. With a familiar reflex, I close the kettle's lid, find the "boil" button on the handle by feel alone, and place the water pitcher near the sink. I grab the cup used for measuring beans and the water spritzer. It's empty. It's empty because I washed it three days ago.

*Full breaths. Feel your feet planted on the floor.*

I fill the spritzer with water and prime the pump with a few sprays into the sink. I measure out 50 grams of beans. Normally, I'd try to get more precisely to 48 grams, but I figure two grams is negligible to the 800 milliliter pot I'm about to make. As practiced for several years, I spritz the beans three or four times, then shake the cup like I'm playing Yahtzee. I dump the cup into the grinder and turn it on.

*You've cleaned the counters thoroughly. It's safe to prepare food here.*

The earthenware colander is not in its usual place across the counter on the other side of the sink: it's on the table.  I find it among the other furiously cleaned dishes in the designated "known clean" area outside the kitchen. I take a couple handfuls of blueberries from the plastic carton and put them in the colander before running it under water.

*Breathe deeply. Feel the air entering your nostrils, moving through your sinuses, and into your lungs.*

The granola is in the living room in a plastic trash bag that contains food packaging that I wiped down with Clorox wipes. Unnecessary packaging was thrown out, so there are unidentified cracker bags among a some rigid plastic containers. I grab the container with my granola and head back to the kitchen.

*Lungs are incredibly delicate yet robust. Consider the air touching the alveoli and exchanging oxygen with your body. Consider the wild reality that your body uses rust to take in oxygen.*

I pour some granola into the yogurt container, then dump the colander of blueberries on top. Rinsed blueberries always go on top–the water makes the granola just a little less crunchy without making anything soggy. The grinder's sound has become regular indicating no more beans are in the hopper. The water is just about boiling. I pull a dripper from the tray and a fresh V60 filter and place it on top of my pot.

*Literal physical phenomena is responsible for the mechanism your body relies on most to stay alive. Your diaphragm creates a light vacuum in your chest, causing your lungs to stretch with the growing cavity, and that causes a pressure difference between inside your lungs and outside your body.*

The focus starts to return to my vision. I pour boiling water over the coffee grounds until the scale reads about 160 grams. I go back to the table to get my tray: the tray Bonnie got for me from an artisan on Etsy years ago. I use it nearly every workday to curry my breakfast and coffee from the kitchen upstairs to the office. I pour another 200 grams of water over the grounds. 

*The exterminator is coming today. They will patch the holes and place traps and handle them if–and when–they catch another rat.*

I pour another two times until I reach 800 grams. The trashcan is behind me instead of under the sink. Normally, I would pick up the dripper in a swift motion into the sink, but today I carry the pot and dripper over to the trash. With one hand, I manage to extricate the filter with the wet grounds and drop it into the empty trashcan. I take the pot and dripper back to the sink in order to place the dripper into the sink. I screw the lid onto the pot and place it on my tray. I rinse the dripper and place it in the dish rack.

Work is going to be busy today. I carry my breakfast and coffee tray up to the office and get to work.

Panic attacks fucking suck.
